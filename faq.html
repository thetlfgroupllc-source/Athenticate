01 Anonymity
Can my agent find out I reviewed them?

No. Your name, team, and position never appear anywhere on the platform, and there's no admin function that would let an agent request reviewer identities. What gets published is your league, your star ratings, your written review, and the tags you pick. That's the whole list. You verify once. After that, you're anonymous on every review you leave.

On the backend, your name isn't stored on your review. It sits in a separate verification record, referenced only through controlled internal processes tied to specific integrity functions (duplicate prevention, update requests, responding to a flagged review). No agent-facing tool, admin dashboard, or public surface exposes reviewer names to anyone. That's the architecture we built on purpose.

What actually shows up on a published review?

Your league. Your star ratings across each category. The written portion of your review. The tags you picked during submission. Nothing else.

No year, no team, no position, no contract size, no signing window, no handle, no initials, no timestamp. If a detail could narrow the pool of possible authors, it doesn't get published.

Who can actually read my review?

Other players. That's it. Reviews live inside the player community, not on the public internet. Reading the full review text requires either a verified athlete subscription or access through a college that has licensed the platform on behalf of its athletes.

Journalists, agents who haven't claimed and subscribed, fans, and anyone outside the verified community sees only the aggregate rating and review count on each agent's profile. Not the words. Not the categories. Not the tags. The detailed content stays inside the wall.

If an agent wants to read what their former clients are saying about them, they have to claim their profile and subscribe. They never see who wrote what — they just see the same anonymous review text any verified athlete sees.

What if an agency or league tries to subpoena my identity?

This is the question that sits closest to the real fear, so we'll answer it plainly. The review itself doesn't carry your name, and we hold less than you might think. What's stored is separated, minimized, and unreachable through any product or admin feature an agent or agency could use.

For what we do hold, the commitment is to challenge legal process where there are grounds, notify the affected reviewer when the law allows, and publish the outcome. The full legal response policy will be posted before the first sport goes live, so you can read exactly where we stand before you submit.

02 Verification
How do you verify I'm actually a professional athlete?

Verification runs against official league rosters and the same public records leagues and teams already maintain. Once you're confirmed as a pro, we store only what's needed to prevent duplicate accounts and discard the rest. The verification record and the review record are kept separate inside the platform. That's an architecture decision, not a policy one.

The process is a one-time check: roster match plus a basic identity confirmation. No tax documents, no contract uploads, no personal financials. If your league isn't live yet, you can join the waitlist and we'll verify you when it opens.

Which leagues and levels are eligible?

Any current or former player on an official roster of a recognized professional league counts as eligible. Practice squad and two-way contracts count. International and developmental leagues count if they maintain verifiable rosters. College athletes who have signed with an agent to navigate the draft also qualify.

Leagues unlock one at a time. The waitlist on the homepage is the fastest way to find out when yours is live.

Why require verification at all? Doesn't that create risk?

Fair concern. Verification exists for one reason: without it, the reviews are worth nothing. A platform anyone can post on is a platform no one trusts. Players reading reviews need to know the signal is coming from other players, not from competing agencies, disgruntled ex-employees, or bots.

The risk you're pointing at is real, and it's exactly why verification and reviews live in separate systems. Your name isn't on your review. It sits in the verification record, referenced only through controlled internal processes. Confirming you're a pro and publishing what you wrote are handled as two separate problems, with two separate data paths.

03 Legal
Can I get sued for leaving a negative review?

Honest reviews of your own experience carry the same protections that apply on every review platform. What isn't protected is stating something as fact when you don't actually know it's true. Describe what you've lived through or are living through and how it's affected you. Don't make claims about things you didn't witness firsthand. Your identity is also never displayed publicly, so there's no named reviewer for anyone to target in the first place.

If an agent disputes a review's factual accuracy, they can flag it. Flagged reviews stay published unless there's evidence the claim is false. We don't pull reviews because an agent is upset. We pull them when something specific and verifiable is wrong.

What's the line between opinion and something I could get in trouble for?

Opinion and lived experience are almost always protected. "He doesn't return my calls for weeks at a time" is your experience. "He feels distracted and disengaged" is your opinion. Both belong on the platform.

Where it gets risky: stating a fact you don't actually know. "He stole money from me" is a factual claim that has to be true, and provable, to sit in that form. The simple version: write what's happened or is happening to you, not what you suspect happened to someone else. If you're unsure whether a line crosses over, rephrase it as your experience rather than a fact about them.

04 The Review Itself
Will you edit or censor what I write?

No. We don't soften tone, rewrite phrasing, or trim content because an agent looks bad in it. The only things that get removed are specific identifying details about you (if you accidentally include a team, a game, or a year that narrows the pool), slurs or personal attacks unrelated to the agent relationship, and factual claims that turn out to be false.

A review that makes an agent look terrible and is also accurate gets published as written. That's the entire point of the platform.

Can I update a review later?

Yes. Agents change. The platform reflects that. If your assessment of the relationship evolves — better or worse — you can update your ratings, your tags, and your written review. The current version is what other players see, marked with a "last updated" date so anyone reading knows the assessment was revised.

For now, updates are handled through email at admin@athenticate.org. We verify the match internally and apply the change. A self-service update flow ships before public launch.

Can I leave more than one review of the same agent?

Yes, with limits. Up to three reviews per agent over the lifetime of the relationship, with at least twelve months between reviews. The reasoning: a single review can't capture a five-year relationship that started one way and ended another. Rookie year, second contract, and end of career are three different perspectives, and they're all worth publishing.

Each review carries its own date and stands on its own. Other players reading the agent's profile see the chronology, not just one snapshot.

Can an agent pay to remove a bad review?

No. Never. Full stop. There's no tier of paid access that affects moderation. An agent cannot pay to remove reviews, reorder reviews, bury reviews, or influence the rating average. The only way a review comes down is if it's demonstrably false or violates the content rules, and that applies identically whether the agent is a top-five name or has never been heard of before.

If that ever changes, the platform stops being worth anything, and we know it.

Is leaving a review free?

Yes. Reviewing is always free, for every athlete. Verification is free. Submitting reviews is free. Updating your reviews is free. The platform's value comes from athletes being heard, and putting a price on that would defeat the purpose.

Reading the full review database does require a subscription or institutional license, and that's where the platform's revenue comes from. But the act of contributing your experience never costs you anything.

05 Agents and What They See
Can the agent I'm reviewing read what I wrote?

If they claim their profile and pay for access, yes — they can read the words. They cannot see who wrote them. No name, no team, no year, no metadata on any review. The same anonymous content other verified athletes see is what the agent sees.

Agents who don't claim their profile see nothing — not even an aggregate of their own ratings on their dashboard. The information about their own reputation is gated behind their own engagement with the platform. Your review reaches them only if they've shown up to read it.

Review anonymity stays in place permanently. It does not change if you later shortlist that agent, open communication with them, or sign with them. Your review is decoupled from your identity for the lifetime of the account. (Profile sharing during a conversation is a separate, opt-in action — see Section 06 for how that works.)

Can agents reply to my review?

No. Agents have no public response mechanism on this platform — at any tier, ever. Their reputation is determined by the players who worked with them. Their only path to a better rating is to treat clients better.

This was a hard design choice. Public agent replies would corrupt the platform: athletes would self-censor knowing the agent gets to clap back, and reviews would turn into he-said-she-said arguments. The accountability loop only works if athletes can speak without a counter-narrative shaping how their words are read.

Does my review affect the agent's ability to find new clients?

It might, and that's the point. The platform exists to give athletes information before they sign with an agent. If your review reflects real problems with how an agent handled your relationship, future athletes deserve to know that before they make their own decision.

What it won't do: get an agent decertified, end their career, or cause specific punishment. ATHenticate is not a regulatory body. It's a transparency tool. The consequence of bad reviews is that the agent has to earn a better reputation by treating their next clients better. That's it.

Can agents pay for better placement, top rankings, or featured spots?

No. Agents pay to see information about themselves. Agents never pay to influence what athletes see. No paid search placement, no boosted profiles, no "recommended" badges, no advertising slots. Search results are sorted by athlete-facing criteria — rating, review recency, alphabetical — never by which agent paid more.

An agent who has claimed their profile and engaged with the platform shows an "Engaged" badge. That's a signal of accountability — they're paying attention to their reviews — not a quality endorsement and not a ranking bump.

06 The Shortlist & Communication
Can agents contact me out of the blue on the platform?

No. Cold outreach does not exist on this platform. Agents cannot send the first message to any athlete, ever — not at any tier, not for any reason. The only way an agent can message you is if you put them on your shortlist and then choose to open communication. You initiate. They respond.

This is enforced by the platform itself, not by policy. The messaging channel is locked on the agent's side until you unlock it.

Can I see which agents are watching me?

Yes. Every agent who adds you to their watchlist is visible to you — by name, with their full review profile. You see who's tracking you before any contact happens. The agent's interest is the signal, and you decide what to do with it.

This is by design. Right now, agents already track prospects through coaches, trainers, and family — you just don't get to see who. ATHenticate flips that: the agent's expressed interest is on the record, and you can read their reviews before they ever try to reach you. You're in the chooser's seat from the start.

Agents only see a count of athletes shortlisting them — never names, never identifiers. The visibility is one-way in your favor.

If I shortlist an agent, do they know?

Not by name, no. Agents see a number — the count of athletes who have shortlisted them — but they never see who you are unless you decide to open communication. Adding an agent to your shortlist is a private research move on your end.

If an agent had you on their watchlist and you shortlist them back, they get an alert that a watched athlete shortlisted them. But the alert still doesn't tell them who you are — only that interest is mutual. Your identity stays yours until you decide to share it.

What does "opening communication" actually do?

Opening communication is the moment you decide an agent on your shortlist is worth talking to. It unlocks two things, and only those two: secure messaging inside the platform, and the ability to schedule a video call against availability you set. No phone numbers exchanged, no personal email shared, no contact outside ATHenticate.

Your profile is already visible to subscribed agents by default (or hidden, if you've chosen Stealth Mode). Opening communication doesn't change what an agent can see about you on your profile — it changes whether they can talk to you at all.

You can open communication with one agent or several. There's no limit on your side and no penalty for opening with multiple agents and ultimately picking one.

Can I shut down a conversation if I change my mind?

Yes. Instantly. You can mute, close, or end any conversation at any point with one tap, no penalty, no explanation required. The agent sees that the conversation has been closed. They do not see why, they do not get a chance to send a "wait, please reconsider" message, and they cannot reopen it from their side.

If you close a conversation, the agent goes back to the locked state. They cannot message you again unless you open a new conversation yourself.

Can agents see my stats, film, or personal info before I open communication?

Yes — and that's the point. Whatever you put on your profile is visible to subscribed agents, so they can recruit you on the merits of your actual work. The platform exists so good agents can find good players based on tape and stats, not based on who has the most connected handler.

You decide what goes on your profile. If you want to be recruited based on your numbers and film, put them up. The reviews you've left as a verified pro stay anonymous and decoupled from your athlete profile — those are two separate systems.

If you want to stay below radar, switch your profile to Stealth Mode. Stealth profiles show only your name, league, and position to agents — film, stats, and preferences stay hidden until you open communication. You give up some recruitment surface area, but you control every disclosure.

Personal contact info (phone, email, address) is never on your profile and is never shared, regardless of mode. Communication runs inside the platform until you choose otherwise.

Are agents going to spam me with video call requests?

No. Agents request slots only against availability windows you set, and only after you've opened communication with them. If you don't post availability, no requests come through. If you do, the requests fit inside the windows you defined. You confirm or decline each one.

On top of that, every Elite-tier agent has a hard weekly cap on how many active conversations they can carry. The platform is designed to discourage volume hustle and reward agents who actually pay attention to the players they're talking to.

07 The Platform
Who's behind ATHenticate?

Built by a former pro who saw the agent accountability gap from inside the system and got tired of watching the next generation of players walk into the same traps. The platform was built from real representation experience and from watching how often the same patterns showed up in conversations with other players across the league.

Full team and advisory details will be published alongside the first sport launch.

No agent, agency, or league has equity, influence, or editorial input on this platform. That's a hard line.

If my school licenses ATHenticate, can they see my reviews or activity?

No. Schools never see individual reviews, individual activity, or which agents you're researching. The same anonymity rules that protect a verified pro athlete protect a verified college athlete on a school-licensed account.

What schools see is aggregate, anonymized usage data — how many of their athletes have active accounts, broad engagement metrics, NIL-related compliance reporting. Nothing that identifies what any single athlete is doing on the platform. The school pays for access on your behalf. They don't get a window into your account in exchange.
